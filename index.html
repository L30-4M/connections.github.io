Solving NYT Connections with Word Embeddings: A Probabilistic Approach

This work explores how pre-trained word embeddings can be reinterpreted through the lens of latent topic modeling to solve semantic grouping puzzles—without any task-specific training data.

The New York Times Connections puzzle presents a deceptively simple challenge: given 16 words arranged in a grid, partition them into four groups of four, where each group shares a common theme. What makes this puzzle interesting from a machine learning perspective is that it requires reasoning about semantic relationships that are often subtle, context-dependent, or based on wordplay rather than literal meaning.

At first glance, this seems like a problem tailor-made for large language models—and indeed, modern LLMs can solve many Connections puzzles through in-context reasoning. But could a solver be built using only pre-trained word embeddings and a principled probabilistic framework? The answer, it turns out, is yes—and the approach reveals something interesting about the latent structure hidden in word vectors.

Defining the Problem Probabilistically

When presented with two potential groups—say, Group A: arrival, flight, gate, destination versus Group B: arrival, almanac, tan, team—you can intuitively tell that one is more likely to be an actual Connections category. This intuition suggests that a probability can be assigned to each potential grouping, and by extension, to each potential solution (a partition of all 16 words into four groups).

This can be formalized as:

$$P(S) = P(G_1, G_2, G_3, G_4)$$

where $S$ is a solution consisting of four groups. Two key assumptions are made: first, that the most likely solution under this model corresponds to the true solution; and second, that the joint probability can be factored using a mean-field approximation:

$$P(S) \approx \prod_{G \in S} P(G)$$

This factorization assumes that groups are approximately independent—not strictly true, but sufficient for finding good solutions in practice.

A Generative Model for Puzzle Creation

So how do we express $P(G)$? To figure this out, let's imagine how a puzzle creator might actually construct a Connections puzzle. A reasonable guess:

Hypothesized Generative Process
  1. The puzzle creator chooses a topic
  2. They choose four words associated with that topic
  3. They repeat this three more times

Now, when picking a topic, some topics are clearly more likely than others. A category like "sports" is far more probable in a mainstream puzzle than "quantum topology." This suggests topics are drawn from some distribution—call it $P(t|\text{NYT})$. Similarly, if I asked you to say the first word that comes to mind when I say "sports," "ball" is far more likely than "poetry." This tells us there exists a distribution $P(w|t)$ that captures our abstract notion of word-topic relatedness.

Taking this reasoning, the unnormalized posterior for a group given a topic can be expressed as:

$$P_N(G, t) = P(t) \prod_{w \in G} P(w|t)$$

However, this doesn't actually capture the fact that only these four words were generated by this topic. You can verify this yourself: $\sum_{|G|=4} P_N(G|t) \neq 1$. To account for this constraint, we introduce a normalization factor $K_t = \left(\sum_{|G|=4} P_N(G|t)\right)^{-1}$, giving us:

$$P(G, t) = K_t \cdot P_N(G, t)$$

The final group probability marginalizes over all possible topics:

$$P(G) = \sum_{\text{topics}} P(G, t)$$

From Theory to Word Vectors

Throughout this framework, one crucial detail has been left out: how to actually find $P(w|t)$ and $P(t)$?

The simplest solution would be to have puzzle creators directly rank word-topic similarities—clean data, but entirely unfeasible. Historical NYT Connections puzzles could be analyzed, but this dataset is small and uninformative since solutions aren't repeated. Crowdsourcing similarity judgments is promising but outside the scope of this project. Existing similarity datasets aren't sufficient for these purposes either.

The remaining approach is to use text data, like Wikipedia, to approximate $P(w|t)$ and $P(t)$. In order to do this, the following simplifying assumptions are made:

  • That $P(w|t)$ learned from text approximates the "true" word-topic distribution
  • That grammatical structure has minimal effect on $P(w|t)$
  • That each passage has a dominant topic occurring with probability $P(t)$

Key insight: The idea behind using text data to approximate $P(w|t)$ isn't entirely far-fetched. For example, if you want a passage to convey sadness, you're more likely to use words like "blue," "rain," and "tears." And any word vectors found from factorizing the co-occurrence matrix will be equivalent up to a rotation to $W_i = \sum_{\text{topics}} P(w|t)\sqrt{P(t)} \cdot \hat{t}$.

Constructing and factorizing a custom pairwise joint probability matrix is outside the scope of this project. In its place, I chose to use GloVe vectors, with the assumption that the word vectors take on the form:

$$\vec{w} \approx \sum_{\text{topics}} \left(-1 + \frac{P(w|t)}{P(w)}\right) C_t \cdot \hat{t}$$

where $C_t$ is some topic-dependent scaling constant. However, there is no way to uniquely determine $C_t$ from word vectors alone without further simplifying assumptions or prior information about $P(w|t)$. This limitation will be revisited later.

Justification for this assumption

It is likely that GloVe factorizes something related to the PMI (pointwise mutual information) matrix. This isn't unfounded—there's evidence that methods like SGNS factorize the PMI matrix, and that representations are roughly isomorphic for large enough datasets. GloVe can adopt the form of a PMI matrix since it factorizes $w_i \cdot w_j = \ln(\text{co-occurrences}_{ij}) + b_i + b_j$.

As for the expression directly, it can be derived as follows: $\text{PMI}_{ij} = \log P(w_i, w_j) - \log(P(w_i)P(w_j))$, and $\log P(w_i, w_j) = \log\left(\sum_t P(w_i|t)P(w_j|t)P(t)\right)$.

Let $P(w_k|t) = P(w_k) + \delta_{kt}$, where $\sum_k \delta_{kt} = 0$ and $\sum_t \delta_{kt}P(t) = 0$ (derived from $\sum_k P(w_k|t) = 1$ and $\sum_t P(w_k|t)P(t) = P(w_k)$ respectively).

Expanding, this yields $\log P(w_i, w_j) = \log\left(P(w_i)P(w_j) + \sum_t \delta_{it}\delta_{jt}P(t)\right)$. Given that $\log(1+x) \approx x$ when $x$ is small, this can be expressed as:

$$\text{PMI} \approx \frac{\sum_t \delta_{it}\delta_{jt}P(t)}{P(w_i)P(w_j)}$$

As for the constant $C$ shift that can arise from $b_k$, its effect on factorization is negligible with $\vec{w}^* = \vec{w} + \sqrt{C} \cdot \hat{b}$. Given the factorization method used, it can be ignored.

Extracting Topic Vectors

The following algorithm is proposed for finding a word's latent topic vectors:

Topic Vector Extraction
  1. For a target word $w$, find the top-$k$ words by cosine similarity in the embedding space
  2. Apply sparse decomposition (SVD with L1 regularization) to find a topic direction $\hat{t}$
  3. Project out the discovered topic: $w_{k+1} = w_k - \langle w_k, \hat{t}_k \rangle \hat{t}_k$
  4. Repeat to extract multiple topic vectors, orthogonalizing against previously found topics

This essentially solves for a unit vector $\hat{t}$ such that the projection $X\hat{t} = \vec{w}$ minimizes both $\sum_i |w_i|$ (sparsity) and the residual variance. Sparsity is needed because it is not guaranteed that all $k$ vectors found correspond to the same topic.

Validating the Decomposition

When all vocabulary words are projected onto an extracted topic vector, a characteristic asymmetric distribution is observed: a bounded negative tail and a long positive tail. This matches the theoretical expectation (up to a constant factor), since:

  • The minimum value is bounded by approximately $-\sqrt{P(t)}$
  • The maximum value is bounded by approximately $\sqrt{P(t)} / \min(P(w))$
  • The weighted mean $\sum_i P(w_i) \langle \vec{w}_i, \hat{t} \rangle = 0$

Crucially, random shuffling of vector components produces a symmetric Gaussian distribution centered at zero—confirming that the extracted topic vectors capture genuine semantic structure, not noise.

The Scoring Function

Since $\log$ is a monotonic function, a solution that maximizes $\log P(S)$ also maximizes $P(S)$—orderings are preserved. This justifies working in log-space, giving us the following:

$$\log P(S) = \sum_{G \in S} \log P(G)$$
$$\log P(G) = \log \left( \sum_{\text{topics}} \exp(\log P(G,t)) \right) \approx \max_{\text{topics}} \log P(G,t)$$
$$\log P(G,t) \approx \sum_{w \in G} \log P(w|t) - \max_{\text{groups}} \log P_N(G|t) + \log P(t)$$

The max approximation is preferred over softmax here because the topic space constructed contains multiple overlapping topic representations, while the topic space assumed in these calculations is the fully spanning orthogonal topic space.

Results

The solver was tested on several Connections puzzles. While the one-shot performance leaves much to be desired, this formulation still holds merit. By examining where the true solution ranks among all 2.6 million possible partitions, there is one key finding: the true solution consistently falls within the top 1% of all solutions by score. This suggests the scoring function captures meaningful structure, even when it doesn't identify the exact solution.

Limitations and Future Directions

This approach has several fundamental limitations, most stemming from the unrecoverable constants discussed earlier.

The $C_t$ and $P(t)$ Problem

As established in the validation, the scaling constant $C_t$ and the topic prior $P(t)$ cannot be recovered from word vectors alone. This forces the assumption of uniform $P(t)$ across topics—a simplification that likely hurts performance on puzzles where the NYT has strong topic preferences.

These issues with not knowing $P(t)$ can in part be alleviated by a multi-shot approach. NYT Connections allows for 4 incorrect guesses, and even tells you when your guess is one away from the true solution—feedback that could be incorporated to refine topic priors iteratively.

Morphological Patterns

Since GloVe does not tokenize its inputs, it ends up missing out on prefix/suffix puzzles like words starting with "RAIN" (rainbow, rainmaker, raincoat) or "COPY" (copyright, copycat, copywriter). This is a fundamental limitation of the word-level embedding approach.

Vocabulary Coverage

GloVe's vocabulary may lack recent celebrities, brands, or cultural references common in NYT puzzles. This is less fundamental—retraining on more recent corpora would help—but illustrates how the method depends on corpus composition.

Despite these limitations, the approach demonstrates that pre-trained word embeddings contain surprisingly rich structure for semantic reasoning. The fact that true solutions consistently rank in the top 1% suggests the probabilistic framework captures something real about word relationships—even without task-specific training or access to the ground-truth distributions $P(w|t)$ and $P(t)$ that would make this problem fully tractable.

Technical Details

For reproducibility, here are the key implementation details:

  • Embeddings: GloVe 300-dimensional vectors, centered by subtracting probability-weighted mean vector
  • Bias removal: Project out dominant eigenvector of $M^T M$
  • Topic extraction: Sparse decomposition with L1 regularization ($\alpha = 0.5$) on top-13 similar words by cosine similarity
  • Topics per word: 30 orthogonal topic vectors extracted per puzzle word
  • Scoring: Log-probability with normalization constant $c_1 = 1$, uniform $P(t)$ assumption
  • Optimization: Dynamic programming over bitmask states to find optimal partition
